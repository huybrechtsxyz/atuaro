What if there aren’t enough clerics to stem the tide of the infection and reinforcements are too far away? What if it kills faster than clerics can heal? They only have a spell slot or two, after all. What if the players bring a beloved, infected NPC to a temple and the magic doesn’t work? What if their Gods can’t/won’t save them? (In my campaign, divine/radiant energy accelerates the disease.) How would the people in that world respond to these developments? Would the clerics give up and sequester themselves in their temples, leaving the common folk to die in the streets - convinced it is an unfortunate necessity to ensure the survival of their religious order? How would they decide who to heal - and what implications does that have for social unrest? Would they attempt to profiteer from the crisis? As the death toll rises, do once-compassionate emissaries of life grow callous and dispassionate? Do they believe the plague to be some sort of divine punishment? Do they work until they collapse from exhaustion and succumb to the illness themselves? Rather than an impediment, Clerics and magic offer fantastic narrative opportunities to create believable characters and compelling plot points.

Pick your Pestilence

Stories need objectives, and a spreading epidemic is no different. Ultimately the end goal is the resolution of the outbreak, but by what means and to what degree that happens is up to you. In nature, diseases may resolve themselves - perhaps some portion of the populace are naturally immune? In D&D, however, you first have to consider what type of campaign you’re running, which will help dictate what kind of pestilence is appropriate.

If you’re running an intrigue campaign, any type of disease will do. I would recommend fashioning it off of a real life disease - perhaps Ebola, or an especially vicious Flu. Players will find themselves attempting to help work with the various factions within the government to effectively keep society from collapsing or implement measures to stop the spread of the disease. Perhaps some factions stand to gain from the outbreak, and will work to impede their progress. Maybe the players have to mend ages-old feuds between factions because they each have a resource necessary to produce the cure in any substantial quantities.

For more combat focused campaign (like mine) consider using another trope - the zombie approach. For my campaign, the plague is called “The Song” and creates unpredictable mutations in those it infects (think Resident Evil meets Cthulhu). This creates opportunities for surprise combat encounters and adds “Walking Dead” style psychological pressure - how long till they “turn”? Who can you trust? How do the people of the city react?

Regardless of what kind of disease, you have to decide three key things:

    What are its symptoms and how do they progress over time

    How is it transmitted, and does that change over time

    Whether there’s a cure, and how it can be cured.

These are important because consistent symptoms are great for those “oh shit” moments - perhaps after making a perception check to scan a room, a player notices that an important NPC accompanying them has a nose bleed…

 

Phases of an Outbreak

From a narrative perspective, there are a couple defining characteristics of how an epidemic / plague plays out in a city. Drawing inspiration from real responses to The Black Plague and the fictional account of Oran in Camus’ “The Plague,” here are a few that I’ve identified, including possible plot elements:

Foreshadowing & the initial outbreak

    “Isolated Incidents”: begin emerging as the players familiarize themselves with a new location, such as rats dying off in the street in large numbers, repeated isolated incidents of passing people exhibiting symptoms.

    WMD: discovery of a BBEG plot to unleash a magical plague upon the populace. Perhaps the “odd job” the players performed is unknowingly a key part of allowing the outbreak to happen (and thus, the players’ responsibility to fix). Or perhaps a racist evil wizard is trying to wipe out all dwarves.

Initial Government Response

    Incompetence: Authorities slow to respond, disregard warnings as “crazy” or melodramatic. Players may encounter someone pleading to be heard by an authority, or may themselves attempt to convince the authorities to do something about the growing problem.

    Denial: Officials are in denial, publicly decrying anyone claiming something is wrong as “instigators” and threatening punishment even as society continues to deteriorate around them. Perhaps out of pure denial or perhaps for deliberate, nefarious ends.

The Tipping Point

    Making it Personal: A player or someone close to them becomes infected with the disease.

    Making it Political: A major NPC or important political figure impacted by the disease.

    The First Domino: A major institution or point of infrastructure collapses - such as a hospital / temple, garbage collection, or major place of learning collapses (literally or figuratively). Perhaps a panicked mob start a fire that burns down a temple, or perhaps the sewers are clogged with the dead and the streets are flooded with sewage, or maybe there’s an altercation with the town guard leading to the deaths of innocent people in a square.

Government Crisis Response

    Quarantine: Perhaps one of the most powerful plot points for any outbreak story, since it enables a whole range of psychological story elements to play with and build a believable world. Often times, people wouldn’t panic until they realize they can’t leave - including your players. It also forces players to actually engage with the disease and confront difficult morally ambiguous choices - trapping people may condemn them, but letting them go may condemn the world.

    Martial Law: Any government’s primary goal is to retain order - just how far would your city’s government go? Do they merely keep the peace, or do they respond with fear - murdering those suspected to be “infected”? What if the lists of the "infected" and political opponents are eerily similar?

    Inaction: Maybe the Government doesn’t do anything at all - perhaps a young Crown Prince is too insulated in his comfortable bubble of luxury to do anything? Perhaps the Grand Duke’s faculties are ailing, and the advisors are seeking to keep the reality from him? What happens as things spin out of control?

Societal Response & Collapse
This is perhaps the MOST interesting part of an entire outbreak, and where the meat of a “story” emerges. I personally believe that good stories are really just great characters in interesting circumstances.

    Fear & Distrust: People respond to crisis differently, and one common response is fear and distrust. This is especially true during an “initial panic” of an epidemic. Family members may ostracise those they feel to be infected. Or there may be a rush on the local markets, creating chaos. Rival factions may emerge, or tensions between existing ones may inflame.

    Altruism and Egoism: In times of hardship, people may sometimes rise to the challenge in a heroic fashion - and that heroism may even come from some of most unexpected places. Perhaps the thieves guild makes an about face, leveraging their extensive network of thieves and smugglers to get medicine and food dispersed throughout the city. Conversely, perhaps the once-beloved monastic order who preaches love and understanding may callously turn their back on the poor. Perhaps the players help organize a ragtag bunch of people to help combat the disease. Perhaps the once-beneficent Arch Wizard, sequestered in his tower, holds the cure but due to a personal tragedy at the hands of panicked crowd refuses to help.

    Confronting Death: of all the ghouls and ghosts in the D&D fantasy world, in the time of an outbreak the most terrifying spectre should be the fatalistic shadow of death hanging over the populace. However, not everyone responds to death the same way - fatalism can be just as liberating as it is oppressive. The “Danse Macabre” or Dance of Death is an allegory that pervaded the culture of late Medieval period Europe - where death was celebrated as the great equalizer of all (due in no small part to The Black Death ravaging 1/3rd of the population). Perhaps, in your D&D world, a sect begins to form worshiping death in a similar way, taking to the streets in elaborate parades and festivities.

    Breakdown of infrastructure: BRING OUT YER DEAD! Burning bodies, filth, and decay are great atmospheric elements to weave into your narratives. A plague stricken city, in the advanced stages of disease, potentially has piles of the dead lining the streets - which may even become impassible due to refuse, and black sticky ichor may form putrescent pools in the town squares. The water from wells may become befouled - perhaps a young dying man, fetching water to sake his dying daughter’s thirst, succumbs to a bloody coughing fit that sends him tumbling into the well, effectively poisoning the water for everyone else. If your city is under quarantine, consider the effects of starvation / hunger as stores and markets are stripped. If the players have horses, it may not be a good idea to leave them unattended - that’s a lot of meat that could fill a lot of empty bellies. If the city has lots of magical elements to its architecture or character (floating magic crystals, etc) consider how these features may fall into disrepair. Perhaps the city is kept under quarantine by an order of Wizards seeking to contain the pestilence which threatens extinction-level devastation with a giant wall of force - but their power is waning as more of their order succumb to the disease.

    Avarice & Market Forces: Some people may look at the plague and see the panic of the masses as a business opportunity. People desperate for a cure are especially susceptible to scams. Plague Doctors are a great example of this - they often knew nothing of medicine even by medieval standards. Any dense city should be full of people promising miracle cures, and these “snake oil” salesmen can even act as plot hooks - perhaps that amulet that wards off the plague the players bought is actually cursed and part of the BBEG’s plan to thwart the players …. Or maybe it works, and the players need to figure out why! Commodity prices are another good way of subtly showing the progression of plague. Imagine the surprise on your players’ faces when horse feed, rations, or potions of healing are suddenly and unexpectedly at a 100% markup.

    Business as Usual: Maybe the people don't notice whats happening, or (more significantly) pretend that everything is / will be fine. This was a core aspect in Camus book, and it simultaneously illuminates two interesting aspects of how common people react to crisis. On the one hand, the willful ignorance/stupidity of the "couldn't happen here" mentality cost many their lives as the plague ran unchecked in the city. On the other hand, maybe carrying on in spite of the horror and capricuous absurdity around you isnt naieve optimism, but rather a kind of heroic determination that reflects the resilience of the human spirit... or maybe that pristine enclave of healthy folk are hiding some dark secret.

Ending the Plague & Twists

    The Cure: if your plague can be cured, having your players harvest/secure enough of a specific resource (or combinations of resources) is the most obvious way to bring the plague to an end. The city is saved, the players are heros. Hooray. Personally, I think this approach is boring.

    Source of Corruption: maybe your plague is actually being caused by some magical source of corruption. If this is the path you’d like to go, think of the search for the cure as a mystery - the players need to find what’s causing it, where it is, and how to stop it. This can make for some rich storytelling opportunities, especially if you pull an M. Knight Shyamalan on them (“The king lied! He’s really a warlock! The “evil” seed was actually containing the spread - the only way to stop it now is to kill the king!”)

    (Un)Natural Selection: One thing about real world plagues is that some people are naturally immune. This is an interesting area to explore when it comes to guiding the players towards resolving the crisis. The players can, for example, spend their time trying to find out what that one thing is that all the people who are unaffected have in common like a type of detective story. Alternatively, perhaps the players discover that the plague was engineered to only target half orcs, deliberately, and the High Council’s quarantine is just a pretext for genocide - we must help them escape the city! Maybe the players really can’t do anything and the plague naturally resolves itself, leaving them to wonder what it was all for....

    12 Monkeys: Honestly, maybe the players just lose. Failing to contain the pestilence is a great way to move on from a city and turn the campaign into a far-flung globetrotting adventure. If the pestilence was the evil plan of the BBEG, maybe the BBEG escapes with the disease. The players, begrudgingly, leave the city behind only to later return to an empty city of the dead filled with vengeful spirits angry at them for their failure (that they now must put to rest). Maybe they’re sent to warn a neighboring town, but can’t make it in time.

    What's your Vector, Victor?: Another way to resolve the plague is to focus on the mode of transmission, rather than an actual cure. The Black Plague was spread mostly by rats - so perhaps rounding up and killing all the rats in town (perhaps with a final confrontation with the dreaded "Rat Queen" in the sewers) could be the way to resolve the plague. Another idea is to have the bizarre disease ravaging the town be linked to that popular, must-have magical trinket that everyone has. Maybe your plague takes place in a goblin city and your players have to go on a quest educating goblins about washing their hands and other utterly foreign hygiene concepts.

 

Infected Players

As a final note, there’s one thing I haven’t really addressed yet - and that’s what to do if the players themselves become infected. In my game, the infection is a pretext for the party gaining warlock-like powers, binding them to a GOO. However, in a place where there’s deadly disease everywhere and people dying in the streets there’s a few ways to handle players contracting the disease.

    The first is to create infection mechanics (such as stages of the disease, with saves to advance or regress the illness) and just play it off that the reason they won’t necessarily die a horrible death like everyone else is because adventurers are basically just stronger than common folk. This can create an added layer of psychological pressure and enhance the “horror” aspect of the game: “Uh oh, Grok has a nosebleed - is he going to go crazy and murder us all? Is it only a matter of time?”

    The second is to make them immune for some unknown reason, and build figuring out the reason directly into the plot. This is neat because it allows for plot hooks, like a King demanding to know why they aren’t infected like everyone else and begging them to help their city.

    The third is to hold off the onset of the disease until the players have access to Lesser Restoration spell that can keep them from dying. If the infection rate of your plague is high, then this can create tense moments where the party has to make difficult choices - do we heal the rogue, or do we heal this vitally important NPC and hope the rogue makes their saves?


To learn more about the plague the players will inevitably wander into a church. The church is full with sick people, a few priests rush about trying to help with mundane methods. Only the high priest knows spells that can cure disease but he only has so many spell slots, so he only cures his priests.

If the party has a cleric or paladin this is the time to tempt them to cure someone. For example a little girl with severe symptoms. If they bite, the other infected notice of course and pile on them, begging for healing, possibly infecting the healer.

A couple of days later the party witnesses an execution in the town square - a group of infected murdered the little girl and drank her blood believing it could cure them. The high priest stands next to the party and says: "This is why I only cure my priests in secrecy."

And what if it worked? Suddenly you're shifting gears from just dealing with a plague to dealing with an intense situation where the sick are hunting down the cured. Is it worth sacrificing a little girl if it saves 10 lives? Should the elderly be cured and eaten because "they've had their time?" Is there a way to pass the immunity without killing? Lots of interesting questions here.

An example maybe of how this would work in a plague situation: In the great plaza outside of the holy Temple, an angry mob has formed. This complicates the fact that the party has been asked to come see the high priest. As the party approaches the mob, they see three things:

    A priest of the temple that was unfortunately caught outside the gates, beleaguered by the poor and desperate.

    A crying little girl kneeling next to the fallen body of her father, trying to shake him awake and get him back on his feet. Both are in danger of being trampled by the press of the throng.

    A crazed-looking man who has found a box to stand head and shoulders above the crowd, spitting invective and attempting to whip the crowd up into a fury.

The party has enough time to push their way through the mob to one of these situations in order to help resolve them (this may not be immediately apparent to them but you can say something like 'you're going to need to stick close to each other if you don't want to get separated, which of these do you want to try to solve first) and after they pick one, show the other two ending badly. Then repeat.

DCO handles all of this with an excellent flowchart (which was a surprise to me, to see a flowchart in an adventure that was so gosh-darn useful!) but you could probably manage it in any number of ways. Other possible situations: a fire which will spread if not brought under control, thieves working the edges of the crowd while the crowd is distracted, an old woman with a deformity being blamed for causing the illness and in danger of being a victim of mob justice...

I'd also recommend throwing a rival adventuring party at the players. Maybe the temple has an artifact which can cure disease, or at least grant advantage on saves vs. the disease - and it's just been stolen by a band of thieves in the employ of the neighboring country, which has also been stricken by plague but has no such artifact and is worried that once the plague passes they will be too weak to withstand the orcs. Can the party catch the thieves before they make it to the border?

Also, if the party is known to the wealthy / well-connected people in the city, maybe offer them some chances to make money in less orthodox ways. Maybe a very wealthy noble is willing to pay large sums for magical disease removal, and the priests of the city are all tied up with treating the poor? Of course when the party gets there, maybe he's only 'sick' with an embarrassing rash, or is only a hypochondriac - can they justify spending their healing on something so petty if the price is right?

Alternately, an associate of the party could point out that 'while all of the guards have the flu' is an excellent time to break into the Merchant Guild's headquarters and steal their paychests - and he just so happens to be willing to sell the party the map or key or other item that they would need to pull off the heist.
